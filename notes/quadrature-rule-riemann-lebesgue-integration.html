Quadrature, Riemann & Lebesgue Integration

Category: Computational Modeling (Numerical Methods)

Last updated: ...

Quadrature Rule

When an analytical solution to an integral is unavailable due to complex geometry or governing equations, we use numerical quadrature. A quadrature rule approximates a definite integral as a weighted sum of function values at specific points (nodes).

\[ \int_{-1}^{1} f(x) \, dx \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i f(x_i) \]

This is the fundamental approach for solving weak forms in the Finite Element Method (FEM), where integrals over elements are evaluated at Gauss points.

Integration Theory

Beyond numerical approximation, it is important to distinguish between different theoretical frameworks of integration:

  • Riemann Integration: The standard approach taught in basic calculus, based on summing the areas of vertical rectangles.
  • Lebesgue Integration: A more powerful theory based on measure theory, which can integrate a broader class of functions and handles limits more robustly.
Gaussian Quadrature.

By choosing the points $x_i$ as the roots of Legendre polynomials, a rule with $n$ points can integrate a polynomial of degree up to $2n-1$ exactly.

Functional Analysis.

Lebesgue integration is essential for defining Hilbert spaces like $L^2(\Omega)$, which form the mathematical foundation for modern numerical analysis and PDE theory.
