Lagrange Multipliers

Category: Numerical Analysis · Constrained Optimization · Finite Element Method

Lagrange Multipliers is a strategy to find local maxima and minima of a function subject to equality constraints. It is widely used in physics and engineering, particularly in the Finite Element Method (FEM) to enforce incompressibility constraints.

Constrained vs. Unconstrained Problems

In an unconstrained problem, you seek to minimize $f(x)$ where $x$ can take any value in the domain. In a constrained problem, you minimize $f(x)$ subject to $g(x) = 0$.

The Method

The core idea is to introduce a new variable $\lambda$ (the Lagrange Multiplier) and define the Lagrangian function:

\[ \mathcal{L}(x, \lambda) = f(x) - \lambda g(x) \]

The optimal points are found where the gradient of the Lagrangian is zero:

\[ \nabla \mathcal{L} = 0 \implies \nabla f(x) = \lambda \nabla g(x) \]
Physical Interpretation.

In mechanics, Lagrange multipliers represent the forces necessary to maintain constraints. For an incompressible fluid, the pressure $p$ acts as the Lagrange multiplier for the divergence-free constraint $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$.

Saddle Point Problems.

Enforcing constraints via Lagrange multipliers leads to saddle-point systems, which require specialized numerical solvers (like Schur complement methods or stabilized finite element pairs).
