Inflation Boundary Layer Meshing

Category: Geometry & Mesh

Last updated: ...

When setting up cases for Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) analysis, capturing the boundary layer accurately is critical. This is typically achieved by incorporating a "prism layer" or "inflation layer" along wall surfaces to resolve the steep velocity gradients in the near-wall region.

Defining Mesh Resolution ($y^+$)

The resolution of the first mesh cell is dictated by the target $y^+$ value—a non-dimensional distance that describes the resolution of the boundary layer relative to the viscous sublayer.

Rule of Thumb.

For high-fidelity RANS simulations using low-Reynolds wall treatments, it is generally recommended to target a $y^+ \approx 1$.

Fundamental Equations

The $y^+$ value is defined as:

\[ y^{+} = \frac{y \cdot u_{\tau}}{\nu} \]

where the friction velocity $u_{\tau}$ is derived from the wall shear stress $\tau_w$:

\[ u_{\tau} = \sqrt{\frac{\tau_w}{\rho}} \]

The wall shear stress can be estimated using the skin friction coefficient $C_f$:

\[ C_f = \frac{0.026}{Re_x^{1/7}}, \quad \tau_w = \frac{1}{2}\rho U^2 C_f \]

Growth and Sizing

Once the first layer thickness ($y$) is determined, the inflation zone is defined by:

  • Number of Layers: Total count of prism layers (usually 5 to 15).
  • Growth Ratio: The factor by which thickness increases (typically 1.1 to 1.3).
  • Total Thickness: The absolute height of the inflation zone, often targeting $y_{total} \approx \delta$ (boundary layer thickness).
