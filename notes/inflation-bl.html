Prism Layer Inflation

When setting up cases for Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) analysis, capturing the boundary layer accurately is critical. This is typically achieved by incorporating a "prism layer" or "inflation layer" along wall surfaces. The goal is to discretize the near-wall region—where velocity gradients are most significant—with a fine mesh resolution.

Defining the Mesh Resolution ($y^+$)

The resolution of the first mesh cell is often dictated by the target $y^+$ value. $y^+$ is a non-dimensional distance that describes the resolution of the boundary layer relative to the viscous sublayer.

💡 Rule of Thumb: For high-fidelity RANS simulations using low-Reynolds wall treatments, it is generally recommended to target a **$y^+ \approx 1$**.

Fundamental Equations

The $y^+$ value is defined as:

$$y^{+} = \frac{y \cdot u_{\tau}}{\nu}$$

Where the friction velocity $u_{\tau}$ is derived from the wall shear stress $\tau_w$:

$$u_{\tau} = \sqrt{\frac{\tau_w}{\rho}}$$

The wall shear stress $\tau_w$ can be estimated using empirical relations for the skin friction coefficient $C_f$. For turbulent flow over a flat plate (1/7th power law):

$$C_f = \frac{0.026}{Re_x^{1/7}}$$ $$\tau_w = \frac{1}{2}\rho U^2 C_f$$

Growth and Sizing

Once the first layer thickness ($y$) is determined, the rest of the inflation layer is defined by:

  • Number of Layers: Total count of prism layers.
  • Growth Ratio: The factor by which each subsequent layer increases in thickness (e.g., 1.2).
  • Total Thickness: The absolute height of the inflation zone.
