Velocity of Fluid

Category: CFD · Physics · Fluid Mechanics

Last updated: ...

In fluid mechanics, the Velocity Field is a vector field that describes the motion of a fluid at a given point in space and time. It is the fundamental kinematic variable in the Navier-Stokes equations.

Descriptions of Motion

There are two primary ways to mathematically characterize fluid motion:

Eulerian vs. Lagrangian.
  • Eulerian Description: Focuses on specific points in space. The velocity is $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}, t)$, viewing the flow as it passes through a fixed control volume.
  • Lagrangian Description: Tracks individual fluid particles. The velocity $\mathbf{u}_P(t)$ follows the material as it deforms and translates.

Properties Derived from Velocity

The spatial derivatives of the velocity field reveal the local behavior of the fluid:

  • Vorticity ($\omega$): The curl of the velocity, representing rotation: $\omega = \nabla \times \mathbf{u}$.
  • Strain Rate ($S$): The symmetric part of the velocity gradient, representing deformation: $S = \frac{1}{2}(\nabla \mathbf{u} + (\nabla \mathbf{u})^T)$.
  • Divergence ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}$): Represents expansion. For incompressible fluids, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$.
Implementation Tip.

In most CFD codes, the velocity field is stored at cell centers (Collocated grids) or on cell faces (Staggered grids). Staggered grids are often used to avoid the checkerboard pressure-velocity decoupling issue.
