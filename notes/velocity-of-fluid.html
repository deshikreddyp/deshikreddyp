Velocity of Fluid

In fluid mechanics, the Velocity Field is a vector field that describes the velocity of a fluid at a given point in space and time.

Eulerian and Lagrangian Descriptions

There are two primary ways to describe the motion of a fluid:

  • Eulerian Description: Focuses on specific locations in space through which the fluid flows. The velocity is expressed as $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}, t)$, where $\mathbf{x}$ is a fixed coordinate.
  • Lagrangian Description: Tracks the motion of individual fluid particles. The velocity of a specific particle $P$ is denoted as $\mathbf{u}_P(t)$.

Velocity Components

The velocity vector $\mathbf{u}$ in Cartesian coordinates is typically represented by its components $(u, v, w)$ along the $(x, y, z)$ axes:

$$\mathbf{u} = u\mathbf{i} + v\mathbf{j} + w\mathbf{k}$$

Properties derived from Velocity

  • Vorticity ($\omega$): The curl of the velocity field, representing the local rotation of fluid elements: $\omega = \nabla \times \mathbf{u}$.
  • Strain Rate Tensor ($S$): Describes the rate of deformation of the fluid: $S = \frac{1}{2}(\nabla \mathbf{u} + (\nabla \mathbf{u})^T)$.
  • Divergence ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}$): Represents the rate of expansion/compression of the fluid. For an incompressible fluid, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$.

💡 Note: The velocity field is the primary variable solved for in the Navier-Stokes equations, alongside pressure.
