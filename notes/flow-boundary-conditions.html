Flow boundary conditions

This page discusses the application of flow boundary conditions in specific computational domains, such as pipe flow where conditions must be prescribed at the inlet and outlet.

Prescribing Velocity at the Inlet

Usually, the inlet condition is known. A Dirichlet boundary condition is commonly employed, requiring both temporal and spatial data. A parabolic flow profile combined with time-varying waveforms is often specified, which proves effective in most contexts. The overall accuracy primarily depends on the boundary condition applied at the unknown (outlet) end.

Prescribing Conditions at the Outlet

At the outlet, one can prescribe either velocity or pressure. For rigid channels, velocity boundary conditions can be used since the volume flow rate remains constant. However, determining the specific flow profile at the outlet can be challenging as it technically lies outside the computational domain.

Popular Boundary Conditions

Commonly used boundary conditions include:

  1. Constant pressure Dirichlet boundary condition
  2. Constant traction boundary condition (Neumann type)

While frequently used in early CFD studies, these conditions often fail to maintain the flow profile shape.

Parametric studies by Liu et al. (2015) evaluated several boundary condition setups:

  • V-NS0 model: Fully developed velocity profile at the inlet; zero normal stress at the outlet.
  • P-V model: Time-dependent pressure at the inlet; adjusted velocity waveform at the outlet.
  • V-P model: Fully developed velocity profile at the inlet; time-dependent pressure at the outlet.
  • P-P model: Time-dependent pressure at both inlet and outlet.
  • P-NS0 model: Time-dependent pressure at the inlet; zero normal stress at the outlet.
💡
Conclusions (In the context of arterial blood flow):
  • The V-P and V-NS0 setups require an accurate spatial profile at the boundaries.
  • While V-P and V-NS0 result in similar velocity fields, the pressure distributions differ significantly. Pressure values in these cases may be misleading.
  • When both velocity waveforms and pressure waveforms are imposed, the velocity waveform exerts a stronger influence on the phasic shape of the pressure distribution.
  • Imposing a velocity waveform at the inlet is generally more stable and robust than using pressure conditions.
  • Hydrodynamic features are more sensitive to the outlet pressure boundary condition in the P-P model compared to the V-P model. Accurate patient-specific outlet pressure data is therefore critical for P-P simulations.

Special Boundary Conditions

While V-P or V-NS0 setups are often preferred, they come with challenges. This leads to the concept of more advanced outflow boundary conditions.

Motivation: At the outlet, simple traction-zero conditions may produce unrealistic results if the domain continues beyond the boundary. The stress tensor includes both viscous and pressure contributions:

$$\sigma = \mu \left( \nabla \mathbf{u} + \left( \nabla \mathbf{u} \right)^T \right) - p \mathbf{I}_d$$

The boundary condition based on the balance of normal forces is:

$$\mu \left( \nabla \mathbf{u} + \left( \nabla \mathbf{u} \right)^T \right) \cdot \mathbf{n} - p \mathbf{n} = - P_{\text{ext}} \mathbf{n}$$

For domains where the fluid continues beyond the boundary (free outlet conditions), a simplified form is often used:

$$\mu \nabla \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n} - p \mathbf{n} = - P_{\text{ext}} \mathbf{n}$$

More Sophisticated Approaches

  1. Backflow Stabilization: For truncated domains like arterial flows, a velocity-dependent traction condition prevents divergence during flow reversal:
    $$\mathbf{t} = \beta \rho (\mathbf{v} \otimes \mathbf{v}) \cdot \mathbf{n}$$
  2. Flow Resistance & Impedance BCs
  3. Windkessel Models
