Classification of PDEs

Category: Numerical Analysis · Mathematical Physics · PDE Theory

Last updated: ...

To solve PDEs, you need to "get the feeling" of the equations. Beyond the standard classifications (linear vs. nonlinear, homogeneous vs. non-homogeneous), there is a much richer classification based on characteristics.

Linearity and Homogeneity

Nonlinear PDEs are usually solved by linearizing them or using Newton-Raphson methods. In a computational sense, solving a nonlinear PDE often just means adding an outer iteration loop. Homogeneous and non-homogeneous equations are structurally similar, so we often focus on the more general non-homogeneous case.

Physical Context

We typically distinguish between Boundary Value Problems (BVP) and Initial Value Problems (IVP) (time evolution). Many physical problems are a mix of both. The reason time is treated as a unique dimension in PDEs is its "one-way" nature; information propagates forward in time, which dictates the choice of numerical schemes (marching vs. steady-state solvers).

Second-Order Linear PDEs

Most governing equations in physics (Heat, Wave, Laplace) are second-order linear PDEs. They are classified into Elliptic, Parabolic, and Hyperbolic based on the sign of the discriminant of their coefficients.

Theory of Characteristics.

This classification determines how information propagates. Hyperbolic equations (wave) have finite propagation speeds, while Elliptic equations (Laplace) propagate information instantaneously across the whole domain.
