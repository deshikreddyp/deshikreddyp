Temperature

Category: Fluid Mechanics · Thermodynamics · Heat Transfer

Last updated: ...

In fluid dynamics, Temperature ($T$) is a scalar field representing the average kinetic energy of the fluid molecules. It is a fundamental thermodynamic variable required to close the system of equations for compressible flows or buoyancy-driven incompressible flows.

The Energy Equation

The transport of temperature is governed by the conservation of energy. For an incompressible fluid with constant properties, the temperature equation is:

\[ \rho C_p \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla T \right) = k \nabla^2 T + \Phi + Q \]

where $k$ is thermal conductivity, $\Phi$ is the viscous dissipation function, and $Q$ represents internal heat sources.

Thermal Diffusivity ($\alpha$).

The rate at which heat spreads through a material is given by: \[ \alpha = \frac{k}{\rho C_p} \]

Coupling via Boussinesq.

In natural convection, temperature changes induce density variations that drive the flow via the buoyancy term in the momentum equation: \[ \mathbf{f}_{buoyancy} = -\rho_0 \beta (T - T_0) \mathbf{g} \]

Viscous Dissipation.

The term $\Phi$ represents the conversion of mechanical energy into heat due to viscosity. It is often negligible except in high-speed aerodynamic flows or extremely viscous fluids (like polymer melts).
