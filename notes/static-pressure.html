Static Pressure

Category: Computational Modeling (Numerical Methods)

Last updated: ...

Static Pressure is the actual thermodynamic pressure of a fluid. It is the internal energy per unit volume, excluding kinetic energy, and represents the pressure felt by a sensor moving exactly with the fluid flow.

Physical Significance

In a fluid at rest, the static pressure is isotropic (acts equally in all directions). In a moving fluid, it represents the potential for the fluid to do work. Unlike dynamic pressure, it does not depend on the observer's relative velocity.

Bernoulli Relation.

For incompressible, frictionless flow, the sum of static and dynamic pressure along a streamline is constant (Total Pressure $p_t$):

\[ p_t = p + \frac{1}{2}\rho u^2 \]

Implementation in CFD

In most incompressible Navier-Stokes solvers, the pressure variable solved for is actually the kinematic pressure ($P$):

\[ P = \frac{p}{\rho} \quad [m^2/s^2] \]

This decoupling of density from the pressure-velocity coupling simplifies the numerical schemes (like SIMPLE or PISO).

Measurement Errors.

Standard pressure probes (like Pitot tubes) measure a combination of static and dynamic pressure. To measure static pressure alone, the port must be perfectly parallel to the flow to avoid "ram air" effects that introduce dynamic pressure into the reading.

Stagnation vs. Static.

Do not confuse static pressure with stagnation pressure. Stagnation pressure is the static pressure the fluid would reach if it were brought to rest isentropically ($u \rightarrow 0$).
