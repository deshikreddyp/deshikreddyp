Static Pressure

Static Pressure is the actual thermodynamic pressure of a fluid. It is the pressure that would be measured by a sensor moving with the fluid flow.

Physical Significance

In a fluid at rest, the static pressure is the only pressure present and is isotropic (acts equally in all directions). In a moving fluid, static pressure represents the internal energy of the fluid per unit volume, excluding the kinetic energy of the bulk motion.

Measurement

Static pressure is typically measured using a static port or a probe that is parallel to the flow direction, ensuring that the measurement is not affected by the fluid's velocity (dynamic pressure).

Relation to Total Pressure

According to Bernoulli's equation, for an incompressible, frictionless flow, the sum of static pressure and dynamic pressure along a streamline is constant, and is called the Total Pressure ($p_t$):

$$p_t = p + \frac{1}{2}\rho u^2$$

In CFD

In most incompressible CFD solvers, the pressure solved for is often the kinematic pressure ($p/\rho$), which has units of $m^2/s^2$. This decouples the density from the pressure-velocity coupling, simplifying the numerical schemes.

💡 Note: Static pressure should not be confused with stagnation pressure, which is the pressure the fluid would attain if it were brought to rest isentropically.
