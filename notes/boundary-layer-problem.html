Boundary Layer Problem

Category: Fluid Mechanics · Heat Transfer · Boundary Layer · Similarity Solutions

Last updated: ...

Historical Context.

The complexity of convective heat transfer arises from the non-linearity of the Navier-Stokes equations and the coupling between velocity and temperature. The boundary-layer concept (Prandtl, 1904) simplifies these equations by neglecting terms that are asymptotically small in thin layers near solid boundaries.

General Solution Procedure

  1. Formulate: Write the full governing equations and boundary conditions.
  2. Simplify: Use physically justified assumptions (e.g., thin-layer approximation) and Scale Analysis to identify and neglect terms of $O(\delta)$.
  3. Categorize: Define limits for dimensionless numbers (e.g., high-Pr or low-Pr) to further simplify the transport behavior.

The Momentum Stage

This typically involves finding a Similarity Solution (like the Blasius solution for a flat plate):

  • Non-dimensionalize coordinates and velocities.
  • Identify a similarity variable $\eta = y \sqrt{U_\infty / (\nu x)}$.
  • Reduce the governing PDEs into an ODE system (e.g., the Blasius equation: $2f''' + f f'' = 0$).

The Energy Stage

Once the velocity profile $u(x,y)$ is known, it serves as the advective field for the temperature equation:

  • Solve the reduced temperature equation (Pohlhausen solution).
  • Determine the Thermal Boundary Layer thickness $\delta_t$ relative to the momentum thickness $\delta$.
  • The ratio is often found to be $\delta / \delta_t \sim Pr^{1/3}$.
Coupling.

For forced convection with constant properties, the velocity field can be solved independently. In natural (free) convection, buoyancy couples the equations, requiring a simultaneous solution.
