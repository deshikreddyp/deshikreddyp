Stokes Equation

The Stokes equation can be derived as the limit of the Navier-Stokes equations as the density \(\rho \to 0\). The incompressibility constraint \(\nabla \cdot u = 0\) is maintained, and the resulting equation is:

\[
-\mu \Delta u + \nabla p = f, \quad \nabla \cdot u = 0 \quad \text{in } \Omega.
\]

This setup naturally lends itself to a variational formulation. Specifically, the Stokes equation is the Euler-Lagrange equation for the minimization problem:

\[
\min_u \left( \frac{1}{2} \int_\Omega \mu |\nabla u|^2 \, dx - \int_\Omega f \cdot u \, dx \right),
\]

where the minimization is over all divergence-free velocity fields \(u\) satisfying the boundary conditions. The pressure \(p\) emerges as a Lagrange multiplier that ensures the incompressibility condition is met.

Proof Sketch for Stokes Equation

  1. Variational Formulation: Define the functional \(J(u) = \frac{1}{2} \int_\Omega \mu |\nabla u|^2 \, dx - \int_\Omega f \cdot u \, dx\).
  1. First Variation: Compute the first variation of \(J(u)\) with respect to \(u\), subject to \(\nabla \cdot u = 0\). This yields:
    \[
    0 = \frac{d}{d\epsilon} J(u + \epsilon v) \bigg|{\epsilon=0} = \int\Omega \mu \nabla u : \nabla v \, dx - \int_\Omega f \cdot v \, dx,
    \]
    for all divergence-free \(v\).
  1. Weak Form: This is equivalent to:
    \[
    \int_\Omega (\mu \Delta u - f) \cdot v \, dx = 0.
    \]
  1. Auxiliary Lemma: By the auxiliary lemma, since \(\int_\Omega (\mu \Delta u - f) \cdot v \, dx = 0\) for all divergence-free \(v\), there exists a pressure \(p\) such that \(\mu \Delta u - f = \nabla p\).

Euler Equation

The Euler equation can be derived as the limit of the Navier-Stokes equations as the viscosity \(\mu \to 0\) and the external force \(f \to 0\). The incompressibility constraint \(\nabla \cdot u = 0\) is again maintained, and the resulting equation is:

\[
\rho (\partial_t u + u \cdot \nabla u) + \nabla p = 0, \quad \nabla \cdot u = 0 \quad \text{in } \Omega.
\]

Arnold's interpretation provides a variational framework for the Euler equation, where the flow map \(\Phi(t, y)\) associated with the velocity field \(u\) is a stationary point of an action functional.

Proof Sketch for Euler Equation

  1. Flow Map and Action Functional: Define the flow map \(\Phi(t, y)\) such that \(\partial_t \Phi(t, y) = u(t, \Phi(t, y))\) and \(\Phi(0, y) = y\). The action functional is:
    \[
    \int_0^T \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} \frac{\rho}{2} |\partial_t \Phi(t, y)|^2 \, dy \, dt,
    \]
    subject to the constraints that \(\Phi(0, y) = y\), \(\Phi(T, y) = \Phi_T(y)\), and \(\det D\Phi(t, y) = 1\).
  1. Admissible Variation: Consider a variation \(\tilde{\Phi}(s, t, y) = \Psi(s, t, \Phi(t, y))\) where \(\Psi(s, t, z)\) is generated by a test vector field \(v(t, z)\). Ensure that \(\tilde{\Phi}(s, t, y)\) satisfies the constraints.
  1. Stationarity Condition: Compute the first variation of the action functional with respect to \(s\) and set it to zero at \(s = 0\), leading to:
    \[
    0 = \int_0^T \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} \rho \partial_t \Phi(0, t, y) \cdot \left( \partial_t v + (u \cdot \nabla) v \right) (t, \Phi(t, y)) \, dy \, dt.
    \]
  1. Weak Form: Reformulate this condition in the weak form:
    \[
    \int_0^T \int_\Omega \left( \partial_t u + (u \cdot \nabla) u \right) \cdot v \, dx \, dt = 0.
    \]
  1. Auxiliary Lemma: Using the auxiliary lemma with Neumann boundary conditions, conclude that there exists a pressure \(p\) such that:
    \[
    \rho (\partial_t u + u \cdot \nabla u) = -\nabla p.
    \]

Summary

  • For the Stokes equation, the pressure \(p\) arises as a Lagrange multiplier enforcing the incompressibility constraint in the variational formulation.
  • For the Euler equation, the pressure \(p\) similarly acts as a Lagrange multiplier in the context of the variational principle described by Arnold's interpretation.

These insights are supported by the auxiliary lemmas that ensure the existence of the pressure term based on the properties of the velocity field and its divergence-free condition.
