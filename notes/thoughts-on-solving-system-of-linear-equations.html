Thoughts on Solving Systems of Linear Equations

Category: Linear Algebra/Solvers

Last updated: ...

Choosing the right solver for a system of linear equations $Ax=b$ is a critical decision in computational science. This note summarizes the trade-offs between direct solvers and iterative solvers, specifically for high-performance Finite Element (FEM) discretizations.

Direct Solvers

Examples: MUMPS, SuperLU_DIST, UMFPACK.

  • Complexity: $O(n^{3/2})$ in 2D and $O(n^2)$ in 3D.
  • Robustness: Will find a solution unless the system is singular.
  • Precision: Computes solutions to machine precision.
  • Speed: Very fast for small to medium problems ($n < 10^5$).

Iterative Solvers (Krylov Methods)

Examples: Conjugate Gradient (CG), GMRES, BiCGSTAB.

  • Complexity: Ideally $O(n)$ with optimal preconditioning (e.g., Algebraic Multigrid - AMG).
  • Trade-offs: Designing an effective preconditioner is often the bottleneck. Mesh quality and connectivity significantly impact convergence rates.
  • Precision: The solution is an approximation; iteration stops when the residual norm falls below a specified tolerance.
Scaling in 3D problems.

For large-scale simulations ($n \geq 10^7$ or 3D meshes with large bandwidth), scaling is the primary constraint. Direct solvers become impractical due to memory growth from fill-in. Preconditioned iterative methods are the only feasible path.
